Johann Sebastian Bach Overview and Wiki

Early Life and Musical Beginnings

Johann Sebastian Bach, born in 1685 in Eisenach, came from a long line of musicians. By age 10, both of his parents had died, and he went to live and study with his brother Johann Christoph in Ohrdruf. Bach’s early exposure to the organ and clavichord set the foundation for his profound musical journey.

Early Career in Arnstadt and Mühlhausen

Johann Sebastian Bach’s professional journey began in 1703, shortly after he left his academic studies in Lüneburg. He secured his first official post at the age of 18 as the organist at the New Church in Arnstadt. This position, though it came with modest responsibilities, allowed him to develop his skills in organ performance and choral coaching.

Bach’s tenure in Arnstadt, however, was marked by controversies, including his extended unauthorized leave to visit the renowned organist Dietrich Buxtehude, which lasted several months beyond the granted period.

These actions, coupled with his critical opinions on the musical capabilities of his choir, eventually strained his relations with the church authorities.

In 1707, Bach moved to Mühlhausen, where his career took a more formal turn. He served at St. Blasius’s Church and significantly enhanced its musical arrangements.

Here, he composed some of his early cantatas, which were more aligned with his growing ambition and musical direction. His efforts in Mühlhausen were well-received, and his reputation as a skilled organist and composer began to solidify.

Weimar Period: Development of a Mature Style

Bach’s appointment in Weimar from 1708 to 1717 as a court organist and concertmaster marked a significant period where his compositional style matured.

His responsibilities included composing and leading performances for the ducal court, and it was here that Bach started integrating international musical forms, particularly from Italy and France.

During this period, he wrote some of his most important works for the organ, including the well-known Toccata and Fugue in D minor, and began exploring orchestral and chamber music.

His mastery of the fugue and his ability to blend his rigorous Lutheran musical roots with more contemporary styles helped him produce a body of work that was both innovative and deeply reflective of his devout beliefs.

Köthen Years: Focusing on Secular and Instrumental Music

From 1717 to 1723, Bach worked in Köthen as Kapellmeister for Prince Leopold of Anhalt-Köthen, who was a Calvinist. Since Calvinist traditions required a more subdued form of church music, Bach focused more on secular and instrumental compositions during these years.

This period was prolific, giving rise to many of his most celebrated secular works, including the Brandenburg Concertos, the Well-Tempered Clavier (Book I), and the Violin Sonatas and Partitas.

This phase of Bach’s life was crucial in developing his instrumental repertoire and understanding of form, which would underpin his later, more complex compositions.

Leipzig Years: Culmination and Late Style

In 1723, Bach moved to Leipzig, taking up one of the most prestigious positions in his career as Thomaskantor, directing music for the St. Thomas Church and School. His role here was comprehensive, involving teaching, composing, performing, and overseeing music across the city’s main churches.

In Leipzig, he composed most of his major liturgical music works, including the St. Matthew Passion, St. John Passion, the Mass in B minor, and numerous cantatas.

His work during this period was characterized by a sophisticated integration of lyrical and structural forms—something that defined his late style. Bach remained in Leipzig until his death in 1750, leaving behind a legacy that cemented his reputation as one of the foremost composers of the Baroque era.

Musical Techniques and Legacy

Bach’s music is renowned for its intellectual depth, technical command, and artistic beauty. He enriched the German style with his skill in counterpoint, harmonic and motivic organization, and the adaptation of rhythms, forms, and textures from abroad.

His contributions to music were both broad and deep, influencing subsequent generations of composers and musicians.

Personal life

On July 7, 1720, while Bach was in Carlsbad with Prince Leopold, his wife, Maria Barbara Bach, passed away unexpectedly. This event plunged Bach into a period of grief. However, life soon brought a new partnership his way.

In 1721, Bach met Anna Magdalena Wilcke, a talented soprano sixteen years his junior who performed at the Köthen court. The connection between them grew, leading to their marriage on December 3, 1721.

FAQs about Johann Sebastian Bach

Who was Johann Sebastian Bach?

He was a renowned Baroque composer and musician born in Eisenach, Germany in 1685. He is celebrated for his instrumental and choral compositions.

What are some of Bach’s most famous compositions?

He composed over 1,000 works, including the Brandenburg Concertos, The Well-Tempered Clavier, and the Mass in B Minor.

Why is Bach considered important?

His music is esteemed for its intricate textures and profound emotional scope. His work embodies the Baroque period’s characteristics and has influenced countless musicians and composers.

What was Bach’s early life like?

Born into a musical family, Bach was orphaned at age 10 and raised by his eldest brother, Johann Christoph. He received his early music training from his family and was a chorister in Lüneburg.

How did Bach’s career start?

He began his career as an organist and later worked as a court musician in several German cities, eventually becoming Cantor at St. Thomas Church in Leipzig.

Did Bach have a family?

He was married twice and had 20 children, many of whom became musicians. Notable among his children are Carl Philipp Emanuel and Johann Christian Bach.

What were some challenges he faced in his career?

Despite his musical genius, he often struggled with the authorities over music and choir standards, and his relationship with some employers was fraught.

Did he have any interests outside of music?

He was deeply interested in the technical aspects of music instruments, often helping to design and repair organs.

What happened during Bach’s later years?

In his later years, Bach’s music became even more complex and profound, culminating in compositions like the Art of Fugue. He struggled with blindness towards the end of his life and died in 1750.

How did he influence future generations of musicians?

His music was revived in the 19th century by Felix Mendelssohn and has since been regarded as a foundation of Western classical music.

What happened on Bach’s musical pilgrimage?

In 1705, he walked 280 miles to Lübeck to study with the composer Dieterich Buxtehude, a trip that significantly influenced his compositional style.

How did Bach’s work get preserved and celebrated?

After his death, his music was somewhat forgotten until a revival in the 19th century. His works were meticulously catalogued in the Bach-Werke-Verzeichnis.
