Randall L Stephenson Political Affiliation

Belatedly in the 1980s through 1990s, Stephenson advanced through a series of leadership positions in finance, including an international project in Mexico City heading SBC’s investment in Telefonos de Mexico (Telmex), where, based on Bloomberg, he was advised by Carlos Slim. In July 2001, he was set a chief financial officer for SBC and senior vice president, helping the company to lower its net debt from $30 billion to almost zero before 2004. He served as chairman of the board of directors for Cingular Wireless from 2003 to 2004. In 2004, he was named as the chief operating officer of SBC and also appointed by President Bush as National Security Telecommunications Advisory Committee.

Stephenson continued as COO following SBC’s investment in AT&T in 2005, liable for all wireless and wireline operations at AT&T. In April 2007, AT&T reported Stephenson would take over from retiring Edward Whitacre as CEO and serve as Chairman and CEO of AT&T Inc. During 2008 Randall Stephenson assisted AT&T launch AT&T Aspire and led their “It Can Wait” campaign. Stephenson was chairperson of the Business Roundtable from 2014 to 2016. In September 2016, Stephenson delivered a speech about race relations at AT&T’s annual Employee Resource Group conference in Dallas.

An employee had posted a video of the speech to YouTube, in which Stephenson asked attendees to make a greater effort to understand each other and communicate better through a persuasive witness defending Black Lives Matter despite racial tensions in the United States. In his tenure as CEO, AT&T acquired DirecTV for $49 billion in July 2015 and Time Warner for $85 billion in June 2018.

Based on Drew FitzGerald of The Wall Street Journal, Stephenson has “changed the phone company he inherited into one of the world’s biggest entertainment companies.”On July 1, 2020, Stephenson retired as CEO of AT&T. He was replaced by then-COO John Stankey. At the time Stephenson reported his departure, it was recognized that the acquisitions of DirectTV and Time Warner had by this point resulted in a massive debt burden of $200 billion for the company, forcing the company to cut back on its capital investments.
